<h3>Chapter Three</h3>
<h3>They say knowledge is power</h3>
</center>

Next morning, after the breakfast rush was done, Dad and I were in the kitchen. I was washing dishes and he was chopping vegetables for lunch. The fire kept us warm and kept the chill outside. No guests to worry about, apart from the obvious, for obvious reasons, but he wasn’t there.

We kept a companionable silence that is only possible when working with someone who knows exactly what is to be done, because they’ve done it a thousand times before. In the kitchen, Dad is a whirlwind. Extremely focused, communicates primarily in eyebrow and hip-bump. He can tell you a haiku with a slight eyebrow raise. And I swear he has two extra arms or something, just for the frying pans. Always a joy to watch a master at work.

Once done, we sat down with hot coffee, and I readied myself for the difficult conversation ahead.

‘Dad, I thought some, and I wanted to speak to you about something.’ I started.

‘No good news has ever been delivered that way. Tear that bandage off.’ He chuckled. ‘Lay it on me.’ 

‘I’ll try and make it quick. Do you remember the promise I made when I was six? The one about lying?’ He chuckled.

‘I do actually, you were so earnest, so determined. Absolutely adorable with those ginger pigtails.’ His voice broke into an imitation tiny me. ‘I love you daddy and I will never lie again!’ He teased. 
‘I didn’t realize you did, too.’ Then he turned thoughtful. ‘I can’t remember you actually lying to me after that.’

‘I have,’ I admitted. ‘But never where it mattered. I learned with other kids, that truth is not always the best answer. Sometimes, truth without kindness is just cruelty.’ I sighed. ‘I paid for that lesson, Dad, but it was worth it.’ His eyes went wide.

‘What happened? Why didn’t you tell me?’

‘Don’t worry, it’s not that serious. Kids being kids, just back then it felt more important.’ He nodded. ‘Back to the matter at hand Dad, please, it’s difficult enough.’

He leaned forward, resting his elbows on the table. That was his tell he was listening, really listening.

‘I remember that solemn promise I made, even if it looked silly to you. Just because I was six when I made it, doesn’t make it any less worth keeping. With all seriousness of that tiny me:’ 

I took a breath and looked him in the eyes. 

‘I saw magic. Real magic.’

_There it is, making Dad choose between what he knows to be true and calling me a liar or being forced to accept what I said._

He closed his eyes, thinking for a moment, took a couple long and slow breaths. When his eyes opened, there were tears. I was stunned. 

_Dad doesn’t cry._ 

He is like a weathered stone on a cliffside beach, solid and indifferent to the wind, rain and sleet. He is permanent, immovable, where everything else is temporary. 

_What have I done?_

He cleared his throat and straightened himself, wiping the tears with his fingers, but they wouldn’t stop. And neither did he.

‘Emma, it is time you learned the proper, ugly truth. Your mother didn’t die in childbirth, like I told you.’ 

I felt my fingers tighten around the tabletop. Now it was my turn to listen. I didn’t know what to say, so I nodded rapidly a few times.

‘She was one of them. Those Outists. She could do things you would think impossible. I never knew that part until she told me, she hid it right until I asked her to marry me. I didn’t care, I loved her so much. She was smart, beautiful and had self-confidence for days. We used to live further west back then, those were three happy years.’ 

He smiled sadly, then visibly braced himself and continued with eyes shut, tears unashamedly streaming.

‘One day she woke me up in the middle of the night, put you in my arms and screamed at me to run. I wanted to argue. It went against everything that I felt I should do. It is a man’s duty to die in defense of his family, if need be. Willingly and with a raised head. But I couldn’t do what she could. She could save you and me, but I couldn’t save her and you. It should have been me. It should always have been me. I sometimes still see her, the final goodbye still in her eyes.’

It hit me like a brick. Immediately regretting every selfish thought from last night, I swiftly walked around the table and took his hands in mine.

‘I’m so sorry! I didn’t know. I didn’t want that. I… What do I do?’ 

I had so many thoughts, they just started coming out. I started crying myself and of course, immediately latched onto him. He hugged me tightly and sat me in his lap, like I was twelve again, and we cried together.

But I couldn’t settle the storm of emotions. Regret and guilt fought fear and irrational anger at being lied to. 

_Why me? Why Mom? I want to have a full family! Why didn’t he tell me? What am I going to do?_

I really needed it all to make sense, and it didn’t. All these thoughts were spinning incoherently in my head. 

And then, there were too many. Many turned into a constant, painful whine. 

I lost control of my body as pain started shooting through my limbs, then to my back. 

I couldn’t scream, I couldn’t breathe. 

I couldn’t close my eyes, saw everything in painful flashes. 

The wall behind Dad. 

The kitchen ceiling, cracking. 

Clattering cutlery.

‘You!’

Two sets of booted feet.

‘I...ave her.’

The table legs, splintered. 

‘All you…is nothing.’

Ceiling again. A man. Hand on my heart.

‘...urt. A lot.’

Golden light, and pain. Sudden, extreme, unbearable pain. With the pain, came clarity. Unwanted and unannounced. It was the absolute worst thing I ever experienced. That new kind of pain, where your bones are making best effort to grind each other to dust, while tendons decide they want to be whips. The kind that settles in the cracks of your soul it creates.

Then, mercifully, darkness.

I came to in my bed, drained and with the ghost of agony lingering in my limbs. Everything was really bright and loud , the downstairs clatter of cutlery particularly grating. By how the light made me squint in my bed, I knew the day was ending.

‘Emma, how are you feeling?’ I heard Dad’s concerned voice. ‘Do you want water?’

I immediately felt how parched my throat was.

‘Yes.’ I croaked.

Dad poured a glass from the bedside table pitcher and handed it to me, supporting my back as I sat up. I drank greedily and sputtered a bit.

‘Easy, easy…’ He wiped my chin and stroked my back. That was the moment I remembered what happened more clearly. I was exhausted and emotional, tears just came.

‘I am sorry, Dad. I wasn’t thinking.’ I hugged him and held on. Feels like home. Feels like safety.

‘Shhhhhh… It’s alright… You just didn’t know.’ I tried to protest.’ Shhhhhhh…It’s okay…’

We sat there for a while as I was calming down, then I realized that both Dad and I were in my room, the bar unattended. I was tired, but not tired enough to not care.

‘Wait, who’s downstairs, tending the bar?’ I immediately asked, alarmed.

‘McCoy and a couple of his friends, actually. Do you remember Miss Ellison from 204? And that young black gentleman, Jim from 206?’

‘Yes? Them? They are the most normal guests we have.’ I started. Then the penny dropped. ‘Do you trust them?’ I asked, incredulous. He didn’t even think for a moment.

‘Yes, once you trust someone with the most precious thing you have and they deliver, what else is there?’ He ruffled my hair. I couldn’t help but smile and tilt my head for more pets. I reconnected with tiny Emma today, she deserved a special moment too.

‘Rest, Daughter Mine, and sleep well.’ I didn’t even wince at that. Not today, nor ever again. Heck, if he decides to call me ‘Sweet Potato’, he earned every right to, years ago.

I slept into the evening, got up somewhat rested and with little pain. I made myself presentable quickly. I needed to know what happened. I forgot to ask, straight on missed the forest for the trees. 

On reaching the second floor gallery, I glanced down to see what was going on. Dad was behind the bar, serving drinks, while our ‘special’ guests sat at a table, seemingly just enjoying themselves. Apparently no need for help anymore, since the dinner rush was over.

It was high time I figured out what was really happening. I had questions. I took the stairs down, hugged Dad and walked to McCoy’s table. As I was closing the short distance, three pairs of piercing eyes watched me. It was eerie, and felt like they were looking at my soul. 

Miss Ellison, unlike her usual, was wearing a flashy wine coloured dress, a bit asian in style, with silvery flower patterns tastefully sprinkled thoughout. It had a big cleavage and a split up to mid-thigh. Normally, it would be vulgar, but she somehow made it look dignified and powerful. Raven black hair flowed down her shoulders and framed her features, makeup and silver jewellery discreetly highlighting the ensemble. 

Jim was wearing a black suit and crisp white shirt, instead of his usual trousers, suspenders and worn flannel. Clean and simple. 

McCoy in his usual, which suprisingly tastefully matched Miss Ellison’s colours. _Ah._

McCoy extended a hand and moved a chair for me to sit. 

‘Sit down, Miss Emma, You look like you have questions.’ He started. I did so, carefully, and watching the trio.

‘Many. Emma Coates, a pleasure to meet you both.’ With all the reassessments I had to make, it felt natural to reintroduce myself.

‘Hannah Ellison, likewise.’ She smiled and extended a graceful hand, which I shook.

‘Jim dus’ Quirrel. Nice to meet you, Emma.’ He switched to first names naturally. I guess the time for formalities was over.

‘Decker McCoy, cowboy extraordinaire.’ I could hear the kick that happened under the table, even though faces remained level all around.

‘What happened to me?’ I fired. 

The other three leaned in. A moment later Dad was standing behind me, hands on the backrest of my chair.

‘I want to know too.’ He said with determination. 

Nods from around the table. Unexpectedly, Miss Ellison answered.

‘It’s called the Twitch. It’s the moment you saw too much to unsee, forget and rationalize. It’s always dangerous, especially so if unsupervised. I would know. You were very lucky you had someone to stop you.’ 

I slowly turned to McCoy.

‘You knew.’ I looked around the table. 

‘You all knew.’ Nods. McCoy shrugged.

‘We came, we saw and we helped.’ Dad wasn’t having any of it.

‘Sure, you helped, but you made it happen in the first place.’ He gritted. McCoy was clearly ready for this.

‘Would you rather it happened when we were elsewhere? When there was no one to help? No one to save your daughter from herself? No? I didn’t think so. And just for that challenge, Mr. Coates, you owe me.’ 

I heard Dad inhale sharply. The other two looked at each other. 

_This man can be vicious when he wants to._ 

‘One day, I will come to collect. You would do well to remember I prefer games where everybody wins.’ 

He looked at me seriously, and glanced up at Dad. 

‘I want you to go to Tucson, Arizona, and find a man named Travis Hanlon. He has experience with this sort of thing. He will be able to help you, if you only explain it to him right. Just, maybe don’t listen to anything he says right after.’ 

His companions exchanged yet another glance. He took out his wallet and set two crisp $100 bills on the table. This is when Dad made his objections known.

‘You expect me to send my daughter half way across the country, just like that? Who even is this Travis Hanlon?’ McCoy smiled, actually smiled.

‘He is my best friend. We have been to hell and back together and I’d rather have no one else at my back.’ Another kick under the table. ‘Apart from , she comes first.’ He said cowering sheepishly, grinning at his partner like he was daring her. He set two more bills on the table. ‘Go and see for yourself. You can tell him I asked nicely that he is a perfect gentleman.’

And so we went.
