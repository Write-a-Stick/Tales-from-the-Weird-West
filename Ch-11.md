That was the day I got a taste of what learning from Decker was going to be like.

Right after breakfast he, Tiffany, Robbie and I took horses from the stable. Decker's horse, in addition to its rider's ego, was additionally burdened with an armful of rifles wrapped in a blanket. We went out of town to a secluded clearing slightly off the beaten road, where a firing range was located. Square wooden braces were set at various distances, thick metal plates hung off beams on ropes or leaned on supports.

We got off the horses, Robbie visibly eager for what was to come. Decker gave him and Tiffany a rifle each, though Robbie's was visibly smaller. They both turned the rifles in their hands, inspecting them with an unexpected level of proficiency.

'Weapon ready.' They said almost in unison. This felt like how I imagined military structure to look like.

'Get the paper targets set up, please.' He handed them some rolled sheets of paper and they left toward the wooden frames, rifles slung over their shoulders. He handed me one. 

'Have you ever used one of these?' He asked.  

This was the exact same model Dad owned, a Winchester Model 1873. I remembered some of the lessons he gave me in the past. 

'Dad showed me how to shoot his a few times. I can't say I'm very good at this.'

He went into the basics, like always assuming it's loaded, never pointing it at anything I didn't want dead.

'Never forget it's an instrument of death and that it wants to kill. Treat it with the respect it deserves. Go on, take the leftmost target, put some bullets in it.' He handed each of us a full box from his saddlebags.

I took my position, loaded the rifle, and started shooting at my target. It was a bit difficult to aim at first, with unexpected booms and cracks coming from Tiffany and Robbie’s weapons startling me at random. Decker corrected each of us, when he saw us do something incorrectly. I found myself making sure I was doing it right, even when I wasn’t the one being specifically addressed. 

'Tighter against your shoulder, Emma. You'll regret it if you don't.'

'Tiffany, open your other eye. Never lose track of your surroundings.'

'Don't yank the trigger. Slower. Pull.'

The most interesting was one he told our little man.

'Well done, Robbie. Very accurate, but slow. Sometimes more bullets in your target faster is what you need.'

'But Uncle Decker, if I miss, the bullet will be wasted.' Robbie argued. 

'No, Robbie. Not wasted. Missing is part of learning how to shoot better. I’d rather you miss a thousand times on the range than once when it really counts.'

I took that one to heart.

Eventually the ammunition was gone and I had to admit I had a good time. The feeling of power and agency in my hands was exhilarating.

We returned to the house for lunch, but had to clean the rifles first. It took the better part of an hour to get them clean enough for Decker.

'Take care of your weapon, and it will take care of you.'

Another one to remember. The man was surprisingly insightful today.

After lunch of beef stew and bread, courtesy of Travis, Decker decided it was time for fencing lessons. I wondered what had made Tiffany hate them so much. Robbie didn’t join us this time. I guessed he was too young. 

'Why fencing? I'm not rich enough to have it as a hobby and I have no intention of chasing trouble like you.' I asked.

'Because trouble may find you regardless, and I’ll be damned if any apprentice of mine dies from anything less than a professional assassin.'

'You're joking! Also, I never agreed to being an apprentice!' I protested.

'Like it or not, you are my apprentice. I know things you need to know and there is no school to learn it at.' I could see his point.  

'You will not leave my care without the ability and force of will needed to choose for yourself. You are already decent with a gun, but sometimes you just need a blade. And that requires a great deal more skill and discipline.'


'  

'Also, You might not know it yet, but you have enemies. Or will. There are those who will try to use you, or kill you, because of what you can do.' It was a chilling revelation.  

In the corner of my eye, I noticed Tiffany stiffen at those words. She suddenly looked a lot more focused. Decker continued what sounded dangerously like a lecture.

''

'What if I decide I had enough and leave?'

'You can leave whenever you want, but you will no longer have the luxury of claiming you didn't know. Your training is for everyone's safety.'

'What do you mean?'

'Look around. It's what I do. I keep people safe. I hold people accountable. But it's not right to hold accountable someone who didn't know any better. '










'Emma, I wish. 

Now, it is customary for the teacher to contemptuously obliterate any hopes the student might have had of their ability with a sword. Lucky for you, it's not a good use of our time. I will forgo my right as long as you take my instructions to heart. Watch.'

He stood in front of the mannequin, drew his sword and cut three times. From the left, right and above. All three target sticks were neatly cut.

'Precision is key. You need to learn to guide your blade correctly. Align the blade with the swing. Otherwise you might as well use a club, it'll be better. Try it.'

I stood in front of the other dummy, pulled the sabre out of the scabbard and took a practice swing. Heavy, difficult to stop and guide. I tried hitting the sticks, and predictably failed. Decker looked thoughtful.

'I see no bad habits, just a novice. Good. The second thing is posture and position. You need to be able to move quickly, know where to move and how to make your opponent follow.'

'That sounds like a very complicated set of dancing steps... How do you keep track of all this?'

'I don't. I don't have to think about edge alignment, or foot position. I already know the deadly dance of steel'

'And I'm guessing it all takes practice?'

'Yes, day in, day out, until your hands and feet bleed. But you don't need to get this good. All I need from you is to be able to fight and win against two unskilled fighters.'

'How long is it going to take? Is it difficult?'

'As long as it needs to, but you're smart, three months should do it.'

If I die by the sword, it won't be due to lack of skill, but contempt for my opponent. I'm going to trip on dogshit and split my head open on a rock.
