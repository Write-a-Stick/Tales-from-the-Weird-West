<h3>Chapter Five</h3>
<h3>Where have I heard that before</h3>
</center>

I couldn’t sleep. Not with Dad like that. At least I learned to breathe. It helps. I can think. He is sleeping now, chest rising and dropping regularly. I hope he will pull through. I know he will. He must. 

<center>
<h4><i>Please don’t leave me.</i></h4>
----------
</center><br>

I watched in silence, counting seconds. Counting helped. As soon as Dad opened his eyes, I forgot the number. He woke up just after dawn, looking around. Then recognition dawned on his face and I released a breath I didn’t know I was holding. He looked a bit surprised when I tried to break his neck with a hug.

‘Good Morning, Daughter Mine.’ He teased like nothing happened. Because for him, nothing did. And it terrified me.

‘I can’t remember… I’m sorry… What’s the result of the entrance test again? Did you get in? I must be still sleepy…’ I knew there was no test. There was no school. Just a world I can never tell my Dad about. I needed time. And I needed it quick.

‘I’ll get some coffee, I’ll tell you once I’m back.’ I didn’t lie. I got out quickly, just to see a well dressed man sitting in a chair right outside. He immediately stood up and grabbed what looked like a doctor’s bag.

‘Doctor Roscoe Endaway, I’m here to see your father, Miss Coates.’ Oh, yes. They told me they’ll send a doctor. I nodded, and backed up, opening the door again. Dad was getting dressed. 

‘Dad, the doctor is here to see you.’ I saw his confusion.

‘Doctor? I’m feeling good. I was just sleeping.’ Luckily, Dr Endaway knew his craft.

‘Doctor Endaway, at your service. It’s good that you’re feeling well, it promises good things after head trauma. You hit your head quite hard. I will let you get dressed.’ 

He sounded professionally soothing while lying through his teeth. This was my moment. Time to think. 

‘I will get that coffee.’ 

I escaped as quickly as I could. I went downstairs and waited for the drinks to be brewed while I collected myself. I was going to have to lie to Dad. But lying is bad. But I have good reasons. I promised him not to. I have to. No. 

Nonono…

‘Breathe.’

In.

Out.

Slower.

In. Out. Slow.

‘Good.’

When I opened my eyes again, the barmaid was watching me with a mix of concern and suspicion. I knew that look. She was figuring out if she needs to watch me closely. Again, I knew exactly what would settle her suspicions. I just had to fake it. It wasn’t lying.

‘I was thinking about… a man.’ Truth. Then I dropped my eyes in shame, she didn’t need to know the reason.

‘Enjoy your coffees!’ She gave me a light wave.

I very slowly went upstairs with the tray, thinking. 

_I didn’t lie. I said no untrue word. I can’t be responsible for what she was thinking, right? Right?_

<h4><i>I HATE THAT IT MAKES SENSE</i></h4>
</center>

<center>----------</center><br>

Dad now thought I was here for school, and I couldn’t tell him the truth. It would make me a terrible daughter, if I now started answering in single words just to avoid it. I owed him every kindness, promises be damned, but unbroken. 

_It’s hard, but as long as I don’t cheat and lie for my own benefit… I can live with that. It’s just better this way._

Now that I knew Dad is going to be fine and himself, I had questions. So I went to find answers. The answers sat down with a bottle of whisky and a snarky attitude.

‘Emma.’

‘Travis. I have questions.’ He nodded.

‘Of course you do. I will answer three.’ I wanted to punch this man.

‘What’s a Twitch?’ One.

‘The name comes from the colloquial name of what you’ve been through. In research documents they call it Overlapping Unified Cognitive Havoc.’ _No. I refuse._ I wanted to hit whomever invented the term with a thesaurus. And a dictionary. Repeatedly.

‘You call it the... OUCH? Do you know what that feels like?’ I still didn’t want to touch that memory. Too raw.

‘Yes, and I don’t care much to feel it ever again. I personally prefer to call it the “Oh-No” or “Just-End-Me-Now”. Most of us go through it once.’ That bit of information wasn’t comforting at all.

‘Most?’

‘Most. That was three questions, Emma. If I decide to be bad at math.’ He put on that smug grin.

‘You know it’s unfair and you are still doing it.’ I protested.

‘Yes, because it amuses me. But my amusement aside, you do have the right to know. One.’ _Thank you Mr Generous._ Still, I needed to know.

‘So how does all this magic work? Is someone finally going to tell me?’ Two.

‘I don’t have that answer, I’m sorry. How it works for you, is not how it works for me. Your magic is natural, built in, so to speak. I only know it’s all about emotions and exceptionally rare.’

‘That’s not very helpful.’

‘I think it will be, one day.’

‘Final question. What is an Outist?’

‘So you have been told something after all. You, me, all of us misfits. We are different and we know it. We see things for what they are. On the outside, looking in - while the inside never even knows windows exist.’

It was strangely comforting to know there were people willing to accept even Decker McCoy and his shenanigans. On the other hand, what have they seen, that made that glittery tornado of contradictions seem tame by comparison?

I said polite goodbyes and left to think it over. Especially the price of knowledge. Did I pay it already, or was there more?

<center>----------</center><br>

The following morning was disrupted by a loud, warbling, mechanical noise outside. Looking out the window I saw a two wheeled  brass and steel contraption, with flames dying inside some pipes. Noticing Decker McCoy get off, I knew it wasn’t a coincidence. I saw him enter the building and sure enough, there was a knock on our door. I opened it to see McCoy in a pose, thrusting his hips to the side and covering his shoulders in a cape flourish. He was holding a cloth covered box.

‘Miss Coates! Good Morning! Is your father in as well?’ He greeted effusively, lifting his hat. ‘May I come in?’

‘No? Yes? Good Morning.’ I stepped aside and motioned him in. I was already thinking of questions to ask.
We sat down, the box on the table. With a gaudy flourish, he removed the cloth, grinning. The box was a small cage, just big enough to contain one You Bastard, in all his stuttering glory.

’Bw…Bwaaak!’ He squawked.

‘He said he missed you, Miss.’ Odd. Normal.

‘Sure he does, you can’t know…’ It clicked. ‘You Bastard? Miss anyone?’ I laughed.

‘His name isn’t You Bastard. It’s Richard Cockburn-Peckersley III Esq. ’ I snorted, completely unladylike.

‘No way. I’m not buying it.’ 

‘It’s true, believe it or not. I helped him pick.’ He grinned. I was already exhausted, and he was here barely half a minute.

‘You need to take him back.’ At that exact moment, You Bastard tilted his head at me and sat lower. He looked a bit pathetic.

‘Why is he looking at me like that? He’s never done it before.’

‘He can understand you now.’

‘How? Why?’

‘I‘m not sure, Miss. Let me ask you this though. Do you have any emotional connection to him?’

‘No. I mean… His stutter was what woke me up in recent years. Every morning I dream of kicking him.’

‘Bwaa?! Pock. Pock bwaak…’

Somehow, You Bastard looked sad. It drooped it’s head, his comb flopping to the side as it sat down, resigned. My heart broke, he looked so sad. I never really hated him, he was just…annoying. 

‘Awwww….’ I couldn’t help it, I reached my hand into the cage and pet his head a few times. You Bastard gave me the cold shoulder, but didn’t peck or startle.

‘There, it’s ok… I don’t hate you. Not really. Not after the mornings, anyway.’

The door opened and I froze. Dad came back. Chicken. McCoy. 

_This is the moment. Truth? No. Misdirection? Misdirection._

‘Welcome back, Dad’ I smiled at him. ‘Mr McCoy brought him over.’ Dad took the room in, closed the door behind him.

‘Good Morning Mr McCoy. Why are you here?’ He sat down at the table with us.

‘I was in still in Canton, when I noticed your friend there looked sad, all on his own. So I brought him over. Look how happy he is to see your daughter!’ Dad thought for a moment.

‘He always did like you more than anyone else…’ _Oh God, he’s considering it._ ‘I’ll get a new one, if you want to keep him with you?’ You Bastard looked at me with hope in his beady eyes.

‘How is this going to go with the…school?’ I asked. And hated myself a little bit for it.

‘No issue, we make allowances for pets. We’re very forward thinking. We think pets are important for student’s development. Some have horses. Others have chickens.’ He shrugged like was normal and obvious.

‘Mr McCoy, you never said you were part of the school.’ Dad was suspicious.

‘I work in the field. Research. Old tombs. Interesting gossip. Someone digs up something valuable? I go and see what it is, what it’s worth. Sometimes that gets me in a spot of bother.’ He grinned. 

This is when Dad asked the question I didn’t expect, and immediately feared the result of.

‘Mr McCoy, does the school have a place for someone like me? I cook, clean, do inventory. I can be useful.’ 

My breath hitched. McCoy, on the other hand, didn’t even blink.

‘We have all the staff we need, and it wouldn’t be fair to fire someone to just give you a job. You can go home. You need to. You have a business to run.’

‘I can sell, reopen here…’ Dad tried.

‘No, conflict of interest. My friend has a pub here, you would be in direct competition.’

Dad looked at me and took a breath.

‘I… can’t leave Emma. She’s all I have.’ He swallowed hard. I held his hand.

‘Dad…’

‘I can’t lose you too…’ I felt tears run down my cheek.

That is when McCoy did something so un-McCoy, it burned into my memory.

‘Science experiments are dangerous. Archeology is, too. Emma needs to learn, grow into herself. We offer it to her, but you cannot follow. Mr Coates, you owe me. I am calling in the debt, now. Go. home.’ He said with a little bit of menace, but quite a lot of warmth. 

‘You need to go home, so she has a real one to come back to.’

‘I won’t be able to protect her there.’ Dad held on, while my heart silently broke inside, holding his hand in mine.

McCoy stood up slowly and made some room around himself. In a dignified, sharp, but not theatrical move he whipped out a sword. A gleaming, mechanical monstrosity of black steel and silver, held along his leg.

‘Hank. While she is with us, I will treat her as if she were my own flesh and blood. This, I swear.’

He cut his pinky finger, saluted and wiped the blade with a white kerchief. He pressed it to stop the bleeding and put the sword away.

I noticed we all stood now, when Dad looked straight into McCoy’s eyes and nodded. That slow, charged, shared nod men sometimes do. That understanding without words. Trust. 

McCoy passed the kerchief to Dad.

‘We are bound in blood now.’ 

Then he bowed, and left.
